## Abstract

Statistical and machine-learning-based downscaling of coarse global reanalyses to kilometer-scale regional fields is an important problem in climate science, with applications to extreme event assessment, agriculture, hydrology, and energy planning. Recent work has demonstrated that diffusion models can generate physically plausible high-resolution atmospheric fields by learning the stochastic residual between a deterministic regression estimate and the true high-resolution output. However, existing approaches operate diffusion processes in full pixel space, incurring substantial computational cost that scales quadratically with output resolution. We introduce **Latent CorrDiff**, a three-stage pipeline that combines the corrective residual decomposition of CorrDiff (Mardani et al., 2025) with the latent-space efficiency of Latent Diffusion Models (Rombach et al., 2022). A deterministic UNet (DRN) first predicts the conditional mean of six CONUS404 surface variables given ERA5 reanalysis input. A variational autoencoder (VAE) compresses the DRN residual from 256×256 pixel space to a 64×64 latent representation. A conditional elucidated diffusion model (EDM) then generates stochastic corrections in this compact latent space, reducing the spatial dimensionality of each denoising step by 16× relative to pixel-space approaches. Applied to ERA5 (0.25°, ~27 km) to CONUS404 (4 km) downscaling over the contiguous United States using 35 years of training data, the model produces ensemble predictions that [RESULTS PENDING].

## 1. Introduction

High-resolution regional climate information is essential for assessing the impacts of climate variability and change on human and natural systems. Dynamical downscaling—driving a limited-area numerical weather prediction model with boundary conditions from a coarser global model—is the established approach for producing kilometer-scale projections but is computationally prohibitive at the ensemble sizes and time horizons required for uncertainty quantification. Statistical downscaling methods are far cheaper but typically lack the ability to generate physically consistent spatial structures at fine scales and cannot represent the stochastic nature of convective events and other mesoscale phenomena.

Recent advances in deep generative modeling, particularly score-based diffusion models, offer a promising middle ground. Mardani et al. (2025) introduced CorrDiff, which decomposes the downscaling problem as $x = \mu(y) + r(y)$, where $\mu(y) = \mathbb{E}[x|y]$ is a deterministic conditional mean predicted by a regression network and $r = x - \mu(y)$ is the stochastic residual generated by a diffusion model. This decomposition is theoretically motivated: the variance of the residual $r$ is provably no greater than the variance of $x$, making the generative task easier. CorrDiff achieves a 652× speedup over the WRF numerical model it emulates while synthesizing variables absent from the coarse input (radar reflectivity).

Despite these advances, existing atmospheric diffusion models operate in full pixel space. CorrDiff runs its diffusion UNet on 448×448 grids; R2-D2 (Lopez-Gomez et al., 2025) uses 340×270. For CONUS-scale 4 km downscaling, the target grid is 1015×1367, making pixel-space diffusion intractable during training. Rombach et al. (2022) showed that for natural images, applying diffusion models in the latent space of a pretrained autoencoder achieves near-equivalent quality at a fraction of the computational cost, with compression ratios of 4-8× being near-optimal.

We combine these insights into **Latent CorrDiff**, which inserts a VAE between the regression and diffusion stages. The diffusion model operates on 64×64 latent codes rather than 256×256 residual fields, reducing the spatial dimensionality of each denoising step by 16×. This enables:
1. Tractable training on CONUS-scale domains without tiling the diffusion step
2. Probabilistic ensemble generation capturing fine-scale atmospheric variability
3. A fully learned pipeline requiring no physics-based model at any stage

Our contributions are:
- The first application of latent-space diffusion to atmospheric downscaling, combining CorrDiff's residual decomposition with LDM's latent compression
- CONUS-scale (4 km) downscaling from ERA5 (~27 km), a domain ~50× larger than Taiwan (CorrDiff) and with higher target resolution than Western US 9 km (R2-D2)
- Simultaneous multi-variable downscaling of six surface fields (2m temperature, 2m dewpoint, 10m U/V wind, surface pressure, precipitation) with a single model

## 2. Related Work

### 2.1 Dynamical Downscaling

Regional Climate Models (RCMs) driven by global reanalyses or Earth System Model (ESM) output via lateral boundary conditions are the standard for high-resolution regional climate information. CONUS404 (Liu et al., 2017) provides a 41-year (1979-2020) 4 km hourly analysis over North America, produced by dynamical downscaling of ERA5 using WRF. This dataset serves as our training target and represents state-of-the-art regional climate information against which our model is evaluated.

### 2.2 Statistical Downscaling

Classical statistical downscaling methods such as BCSD (Wood et al., 2002) and STAR-ESDM (Pierce et al., 2015) use quantile mapping and spatial disaggregation to bias-correct and spatially enhance coarse model output. These methods are computationally efficient but do not generate physically consistent fine-scale spatial structures and cannot represent multivariate dependencies. Lopez-Gomez et al. (2025) show that generative downscaling reduces downscaling CRPS by over 40% relative to such methods.

### 2.3 Deep Learning for Downscaling

CNN-based super-resolution methods (e.g., SRCNN, ESRGAN architectures adapted for weather) improve on interpolation but remain deterministic and cannot represent downscaling uncertainty. Sha et al. (2026) develop a Swin-Transformer-based Limited Area Model (LAM) that autoregressively generates hourly 4 km fields from coarser boundary forcing, demonstrating multi-year stability and future climate generalization. Their approach is deterministic; ensemble diversity arises only from different boundary forcings rather than stochastic generation.

### 2.4 Diffusion Models for Atmospheric Downscaling

**CorrDiff** (Mardani et al., 2025) introduced the two-step corrective decomposition $x = \mu + r$, demonstrating residual variance reduction and synthesizing radar reflectivity from meteorological inputs at 2 km over Taiwan. The diffusion model operates on 448×448 pixel-space residuals, requiring ~12 denoising steps in practice.

**R2-D2** (Lopez-Gomez et al., 2025) uses a two-stage dynamical-generative framework: WRF dynamically downscales ESM output from ~100 km to 45 km, then a diffusion model refines from 45 km to 9 km, generalizing to 7 unseen ESMs after training on one. The first WRF stage, while computationally expensive, homogenizes diverse ESM inputs into a common intermediate representation, simplifying the generative task.

### 2.5 Latent Diffusion Models

Rombach et al. (2022) demonstrated that training diffusion models in the latent space of a pretrained autoencoder achieves near-optimal image synthesis quality at dramatically reduced computational cost. The key insight is that most bits of a natural image correspond to imperceptible high-frequency details; an autoencoder can handle perceptual compression while the diffusion model learns semantic structure. They identify compression ratios of 4-8× (LDM-4 to LDM-8) as near-optimal for natural images. Our VAE uses a 4× spatial compression (256×256 → 64×64), consistent with their finding.

## 3. Data

### 3.1 Training Target: CONUS404

CONUS404 is a 41-year (1979-2020) hourly WRF dynamical downscaling of ERA5 at 4 km resolution over the contiguous United States and southern Canada, produced by the National Center for Atmospheric Research. The dataset uses the WRF model with physics parameterizations tuned for the CONUS domain and has been extensively validated against surface observations. We use six surface variables as training targets:

| Variable | Description | Units | Transform |
|----------|-------------|-------|-----------|
| T2 | 2m temperature | K | none |
| TD2 | 2m dewpoint temperature | K | none |
| U10 | 10m zonal wind | m/s | none |
| V10 | 10m meridional wind | m/s | none |
| PSFC | Surface pressure | Pa | none |
| PREC_ACC_NC | Non-convective precipitation | mm | log(1+x) |

### 3.2 Coarse Input: ERA5

ERA5 (Hersbach et al., 2020) is the ECMWF fifth-generation global reanalysis at 0.25° resolution (~27 km). We use six single-level surface variables as model inputs, matched to the CONUS404 target variables:

| ERA5 Variable | Mapped to | Transform |
|---------------|-----------|-----------|
| t2m | T2 | none |
| d2m | TD2 | none |
| u10 | U10 | none |
| v10 | V10 | none |
| sp | PSFC | none |
| tp | PREC_ACC_NC | log(1+x) |

ERA5 is available at 0.25° resolution on a regular latitude-longitude grid (111 × 235 grid points covering CONUS). Precipitation uses the log(1+x) pretransform to reduce right skew before normalization.

### 3.3 Static Fields

Six static fields are concatenated with ERA5 inputs to provide geographic context that is fixed across time:

| Field | Description | Source |
|-------|-------------|--------|
| Terrain height | Surface elevation (z-scored) | CONUS404 Z level 0 |
| Orographic variance | Subgrid terrain variability | Computed from terrain |
| Latitude | Grid latitude (z-scored) | CONUS404 coordinates |
| Longitude | Grid longitude (z-scored) | CONUS404 coordinates |
| LAI | Leaf area index (z-scored) | ERA5, regridded |
| Land-sea mask | Binary land/ocean indicator | ERA5, thresholded at 0.5 |

### 3.4 Regridding

ERA5 is provided on a regular latitude-longitude grid while CONUS404 uses a WRF Lambert conformal conic projection. We use xESMF bilinear regridding with nearest-neighbor extrapolation to map ERA5 from its native 111×235 grid to the CONUS404 1015×1367 grid. Approximately 32% of CONUS404 pixels fall outside ERA5 coverage bounds and are handled by extrapolation.

### 3.5 Normalization

All variables are normalized to zero mean and unit standard deviation using statistics computed from the training set (1980-2014). The log(1+x) pretransform is applied to precipitation before z-scoring. Static fields are z-scored once at preprocessing time and treated as fixed inputs. Per-variable statistics are stored and applied consistently across all training stages.

### 3.6 Data Split and Patch Sampling

| Split | Years | N years |
|-------|-------|---------|
| Train | 1980–2014 | 35 |
| Validation | 2015–2017 | 3 |
| Test | 2018–2020 | 3 |

Training operates on 256×256 pixel patches (~1024 km × 1024 km at 4 km resolution) randomly sampled from valid spatial origins. A patch origin is valid if the corresponding 256×256 region contains at least 80% land pixels (based on the ERA5 land-sea mask regridded to CONUS404). This yields 87 valid patch origins from ~4,000 possible locations. Two patches per day are sampled during training, for a total of approximately 25,550 samples per epoch. Known fully-NaN days in the CONUS404 dataset (63 days across 1980-2014) are excluded.

## 4. Methods

### 4.1 Overview: Three-Stage Pipeline

Our model decomposes the downscaling problem into three sequential stages, following the corrective residual framework of CorrDiff while adding latent compression:

$$\hat{x} = \underbrace{\mu(y)}_{\text{Stage 1: DRN}} + \underbrace{D\bigl(z_\theta\bigr)}_{\text{Stages 2a+2b: VAE Decode + Diffusion}}$$

where $y$ is the ERA5 input, $\mu(y)$ is the DRN conditional mean prediction, $z_\theta \sim p_\theta(z \mid y, \mu(y))$ is a latent code sampled by the diffusion model, and $D(\cdot)$ is the VAE decoder. During training, residuals $r = x - \mu(y)$ are encoded by the VAE into latent codes $z = E(r)$, and the diffusion model learns to generate latent codes that, when decoded, recover the true residual.

### 4.2 Stage 1: Deterministic Regression Network (DRN)

The DRN is a four-level UNet predicting $\mu(y) = \mathbb{E}[x \mid y]$, the conditional mean of all six CONUS404 variables given ERA5 input. Inputs are ERA5 surface variables concatenated with six static fields along the channel dimension, forming a $(B, 12, 256, 256)$ tensor.

**Architecture:** The encoder applies two residual blocks per level with channel progression 96→192→384→768, with self-attention at the two coarsest levels (32×32 and 16×16). The bottleneck consists of two residual blocks with a self-attention layer between them. The decoder mirrors the encoder with skip connections from corresponding encoder levels. Downsampling uses strided convolution; upsampling uses nearest-neighbor interpolation followed by convolution. Group normalization (32 groups) and SiLU activations are used throughout.

**Parameters:** ~50M

**Loss:** A learnable inverse-variance weighted MSE (Gaussian negative log-likelihood) with per-variable precision weights:
$$\mathcal{L}_\text{DRN} = \frac{1}{V}\sum_{v=1}^V \left[ e^{-\log\sigma_v^2} \cdot \text{MSE}({\hat{\mu}_v, x_v}) + \log\sigma_v^2 \right] + \lambda \mathcal{L}_{\text{L1}}^\text{precip}$$
where $\log\sigma_v^2$ are learnable log-variance parameters per variable and $\mathcal{L}_{\text{L1}}^\text{precip}$ is an additional L1 term on the precipitation channel to sharpen its predictions.

**Training:** AdamW optimizer, initial learning rate $2\times10^{-4}$, linear warmup over 5 epochs, cosine annealing decay, 50 epochs total.

### 4.3 Stage 2a: Variational Autoencoder (VAE)

The VAE compresses DRN residuals from 256×256 pixel space to a 64×64 latent space, a 4× spatial reduction (LDM-4 compression). This is the critical step enabling tractable diffusion on CONUS-scale domains.

**Encoder:** A convolutional network with two downsampling stages: 256×256 → 128×128 → 64×64. The encoder outputs $(\mu_z, \log\sigma_z^2)$ from which latent codes $z \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu_z, \sigma_z^2 I)$ are sampled via the reparameterization trick. Latent channels: 8.

**Decoder:** Mirror of the encoder with upsampling replacing downsampling. Reconstructs a $(B, 6, 256, 256)$ residual field from the $(B, 8, 64, 64)$ latent code.

**Parameters:** ~12M

**Loss:**
$$\mathcal{L}_\text{VAE} = \mathcal{L}_\text{recon} + \beta \cdot D_\text{KL}(q(z|r) \| \mathcal{N}(0, I))$$
The KL weight $\beta$ is annealed from 0 to $10^{-3}$ over the first 30% of training epochs, following Rombach et al.'s finding that very light regularization preserves reconstruction quality while preventing unbounded latent variance.

**Training:** Adam optimizer, initial learning rate $10^{-4}$, 25 epochs.

### 4.4 Stage 2b: Conditional Latent Diffusion Model (EDM)

The diffusion model learns the distribution $p_\theta(z \mid y, \mu(y))$ over residual latent codes conditioned on ERA5 input and DRN prediction. Sampling from this distribution and decoding through the VAE produces a stochastic high-resolution residual correction.

**Conditioning:** All conditioning signals are spatially downsampled to 64×64 and concatenated with the noisy latent code at the UNet input:

| Component | Shape | Description |
|-----------|-------|-------------|
| $z_\text{noisy}$ | $(B, 8, 64, 64)$ | Noisy latent at noise level $\sigma$ |
| ERA5 + static | $(B, 12, 64, 64)$ | ERA5 and static fields, bilinear-downsampled |
| $\mu_z$ | $(B, 8, 64, 64)$ | VAE encoder mean of DRN prediction |
| Position embed | $(B, 2, 64, 64)$ | Normalized (y, x) coordinate grids |
| **Total** | $(B, 30, 64, 64)$ | Concatenated input |

**Architecture:** A four-level UNet with channel progression 128→256→256→512 and 4 residual blocks per level. Noise level $\sigma$ is embedded as sinusoidal features, projected through an MLP, and injected into each residual block via Feature-wise Linear Modulation (FiLM). Self-attention is applied at the three coarsest levels (32×32, 16×16, 8×8). **Parameters:** ~142M.

**Noise schedule:** We use the Elucidated Diffusion Model (EDM) formulation of Karras et al. (2022), a variance-exploding schedule with $\sigma_\text{min}=0.002$, $\sigma_\text{max}=80$, and training noise sampled from $\log\sigma \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu_P, \sigma_P^2)$ with $\mu_P=-0.8$, $\sigma_P=1.0$. The Karras preconditioning is applied:

$$\hat{x}_\theta(z_\sigma; \sigma) = c_\text{skip}(\sigma) \cdot z_\sigma + c_\text{out}(\sigma) \cdot F_\theta(c_\text{in}(\sigma) \cdot z_\sigma;\, c_\text{noise}(\sigma))$$

where $c_\text{skip}$, $c_\text{out}$, $c_\text{in}$, $c_\text{noise}$ are $\sigma$-dependent scaling factors.

**Classifier-free guidance:** During training, the conditioning signal $y$ is dropped with probability $p_\text{uncond}=0.1$, enabling classifier-free guidance at inference with guidance strength $g=0.2$.

**Training:** AdamW optimizer, initial learning rate $2\times10^{-4}$, linear warmup over 5 epochs, cosine warm-restarts every 10 epochs after warmup, gradient accumulation over 4 mini-batches (effective batch size 32), EMA with decay 0.9999, 50 epochs total.

**Sampling:** Heun's 2nd-order deterministic ODE sampler with 32 denoising steps. For ensemble generation, the sampler is run $M$ times independently from different noise seeds.

### 4.5 Full Inference

Given ERA5 input $y$:
1. Regrid ERA5 to the CONUS404 grid; concatenate static fields → $(12, H, W)$
2. **DRN forward pass:** $\mu = \text{DRN}(y_\text{aug})$ → $(6, H, W)$ conditional mean
3. **Diffusion sampling** (repeated $M$ times for ensemble):
   - Downsample conditioning to 64×64
   - Run Heun ODE sampler from $z_T \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma_\text{max}^2 I)$ → $z_0$
   - **VAE decode:** $r = \text{Decoder}(z_0)$ → $(6, H, W)$ residual
   - **Sum:** $\hat{x} = \mu + r$ → $(6, H, W)$ final prediction
4. Denormalize to physical units

## 5. Experimental Setup

### 5.1 Training Procedure

All three stages are trained sequentially: DRN → VAE → Diffusion. Each stage uses the outputs of the previous stage; the DRN and VAE are frozen during diffusion training. Computations run on a single NVIDIA A100 80GB GPU on the NASA Discover system.

**Hardware:** 1× NVIDIA A100 80GB GPU, 8 CPU cores, 12h SLURM walltime per job with automatic checkpoint-based resubmission.

**Data loading:** Pre-processed CONUS404 and ERA5 arrays cached as memory-mapped numpy files enable fast random access without loading full annual arrays to RAM.

### 5.3 Evaluation Metrics

- **RMSE:** Per-variable normalized RMSE on the test set (2018-2020)
- **CRPS:** Continuous Ranked Probability Score across the 32-member ensemble, per variable
- **Spread-skill ratio:** Ensemble standard deviation / RMSE; value of 1 indicates perfect calibration
- **Rank histograms:** Per-variable uniformity of ensemble rank distribution

## 8. Conclusion

We have introduced Latent CorrDiff, a three-stage probabilistic downscaling pipeline that combines residual corrective learning with latent-space diffusion for ERA5-to-CONUS404 downscaling at 4 km resolution over the contiguous United States. By compressing DRN residuals from 256×256 pixel space to a 64×64 latent space before applying the diffusion process, we reduce the spatial dimensionality of each denoising step by 16× relative to pixel-space approaches, making CONUS-scale training tractable on a single GPU. The pipeline is fully learned — requiring no physics-based model at any stage — and generates calibrated probabilistic ensembles from a single coarse ERA5 input.
